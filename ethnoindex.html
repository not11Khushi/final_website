Roots and Resilience:
An Ethnographic Journey into the
Gadhvi Charan & Maldhari Communities

My Role

 I acted as a researcher, listener, and storyteller—bridging the gap between traditional lived experiences and modern documentation methods. This project wasn’t just fieldwork—it was a human connection exercise.
Cultural Interpreter – Translating complex traditions into accessible narratives.

Designer – Framing the findings in ways that could inform both cultural preservation and modern design thinking.
Researcher – Collecting data on rituals, daily practices, education, and governance interactions.

Facilitator – Engaging in conversations across genders and generations.

Why This Place and Culture

The decision to focus on this community was driven by their dual existence—deeply traditional yet subtly adaptive. They maintain their pastoral lifestyle, ritual practices, and strong kinship systems while also engaging with selective aspects of modern life. This tension between preservation and adaptation provides valuable insights for UX research, especially in understanding how tradition influences modern behavior.

Impactful Research Elements

The most powerful aspect of this study was immersive participation—living within the community, engaging in their daily routines, attending rituals, and sharing meals. Over time, barriers dissolved, revealing deeper narratives:

Spiritual Continuity – The miraculous stone of Chandravadi, believed to cure illness and still growing in size, demonstrated how faith binds the community.

Linguistic Connection – Speaking in Tadpadi Gujarati transformed my role from an outsider to “one of their own,” opening doors to authentic conversations.

Philosophy of Relationships Over Wealth – As community elder Rajabhai’s life illustrated, "Life is not about earning money, but about winning hearts."

Cultural Resilience – Even with modern education and resources, they continue to live in Nesdo (traditional row homes) by choice, guided by the belief: "Matrubhumi to maa j che, ne maa ne koi na shode."
